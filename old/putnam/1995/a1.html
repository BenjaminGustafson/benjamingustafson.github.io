Putnam 1995 - A1

Problem

Let $S$ be a set of real numbers which is closed under multiplication (that is, if $a$ and $b$ are in $S$, then so is $ab$). Let $T$ and $U$ be disjoint subsets of $S$ whose union is $S$. Given that the product of any three (not necessarily distinct) elements of $T$ is in $T$ and that the product of any three elements of $U$ is in $U$, show that at least one of the two subsets $T, U$ is closed under multiplication.

Solution

Assume that both $T$ and $U$ are not closed under multiplication. Then there exist $t_1, t_2 \in T$ such that $t_1 t_2 \notin T$ and $u_1, u_2 \in U$ such that $u_1 u_2 \notin U$.

Since $U \subset S$, $u_1, u_2 \in S$. And since $S$ is closed $u_1 u_2 \in S$. Then $u_1 u_2$ must be in either $U$ or $T$ since $U \cup T = S$, but cannot be in both since $U \cap T = \varnothing$. Thus, $u_1 u_2 \in T$.

By the same logic, $t_1 t_2 \in U$. Then since the product of any three elements of $U$ is in $U$, \[ (t_1 t_2) u_1 u_2 \in U. \] And since the product of any three elements of $T$ is in $T$, \[ t_1 t_2 (u_1 u_2) \in T \] This contradicts the assumption that $U \cap T = \varnothing$.
