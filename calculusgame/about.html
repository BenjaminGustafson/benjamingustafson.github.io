A puzzle game that teaches calculus

The goal of this project is to develop a proof-of-concept game where the player solves puzzles, and learns calculus along the way. This game is not intended to replace a calculus course. A calculus course will demand upwards of 100 hours of a student's time, which is too large of a scope for this small game. The game focuses on building intuition for the core concept of Calc I, the derivative.

Students in a calculus course can develop a shallow understanding of the derivative if they focus too much on the algebraic mechanics of taking a derivative. The derivative has an essential graphical interpretation as the slope of the tangent line. The puzzles in the game are based on the relationship between the graph of a function and its derivative.

In a standard puzzle the graph on the left represents some function, $f$, and the graph on the right is its derivative $f'$. The player sets the values of $f'$ by moving the circles on graph on the right. The puzzle is solved by setting the values of $f'$ so that $f$ hits the square targets. We can add more targets (squares) and sliders (circles) so that the graph becomes smoother.

We can use this style of puzzle to teach derivative rules. In the above puzzle, we see that $f$ is quadratic and so $f'$ is linear.

Students are often led to memorize derivative rules. There are popular mnemonics like "fig gif" (f'g + g'f) for the power rule. In my opinion, mnemonics show a lack of depth of understanding. A student should be able to give some explanation as to why each derivative rule holds. A calculus textbook might give proofs of the rules, but these proofs are above the mathematical reading level of the typical student.
